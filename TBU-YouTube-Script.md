### [0:45 — STEP 1: ENGINEER ASSESSMENT AND STRUCTURAL DRAWINGS]

Every basement underpinning project in Toronto starts with a structural engineer — not a contractor, not a salesperson. An engineer.

The engineer visits your home and measures the existing foundation. They look at your footing depth, soil type, any neighbouring structures — especially important if you're in a semi-detached or a row house. They also review your property survey and any original building records from the City.

From that assessment, they produce the sealed structural drawings. These drawings show the pin-section sequence — the exact order in which sections of your foundation will be excavated and poured. They specify the new footing depth, the reinforcement, the soil bearing capacity, and all waterproofing details.

These drawings are not optional. You cannot apply for a permit without them, and no reputable underpinning contractor will quote your job without them either.

Engineering fees for a standard Toronto home typically run $1,500 to $3,000 for the assessment, plus $3,000 to $8,000 for the sealed structural drawings.

---

### [1:45 — STEP 2: CITY OF TORONTO BUILDING PERMIT]

Once the drawings are complete, your contractor submits the permit application through the City of Toronto's eSPACE portal. The package includes the structural drawings, the completed application form, the declared construction value, and Schedule 1 signed by the engineer.

A structural plan examiner reviews the package for Ontario Building Code compliance. Current review times for complex structural permits in Toronto run 6 to 12 weeks. If the application is incomplete, the City issues a deficiency letter and the clock stops until you resubmit.

No excavation. No concrete. No work of any kind starts until the permit is issued and posted on site.

Permit fees for a residential underpinning project in Toronto typically run $2,000 to $4,500 depending on the declared construction value.

---

### [2:30 — STEP 3: SITE PREPARATION AND SECTION MARKING]

With the permit in hand, the crew arrives and preps the site. Dust containment barriers go up between the basement and the living areas of the house. The existing concrete floor is protected where it won't be demolished. Equipment is staged.

Then the crew marks the pin sections directly on the foundation walls. These sections — typically 3 to 4 feet wide each — will be excavated one at a time in a specific sequence designed by the engineer. The sequence is not random. It's engineered to ensure the house remains structurally supported throughout the entire process.

---

### [3:15 — STEP 4: DEMOLITION AND EXCAVATION]

Work begins at section one. The existing concrete floor is broken out at that section and removed. Then the crew excavates beneath the existing footing — digging down to the new target depth specified in the engineer's drawings.

The excavation has to be clean. The bottom of the dig must be solid, undisturbed soil free of loose material. This is the surface that will carry the new concrete footing — and through the footing, the entire load of your house above it.

Each section is excavated one at a time. While one section is open, the sections beside it remain untouched. This is what keeps the house stable.

---

### [4:15 — STEP 5: STRUCTURAL INSPECTIONS]

After the first section is excavated, work stops. Two inspections happen before a single pour of concrete.

First, the structural engineer of record visits the site, reviews the excavation depth, checks the soil conditions, and issues a written report confirming it's safe to proceed. That report is the engineer's stamp of approval on the actual field conditions, not just the drawings.

Second, a City of Toronto building inspector visits and confirms the footing is ready to pour.

Both inspections have to pass before concrete goes in.

On a project with eight pin sections, the first section requires both inspections. After that, the engineer's on-site inspection is no longer required for each section — but City inspections continue at key milestones throughout the project.

---

### [5:00 — STEP 6: CONCRETE FORMING AND POUR]

With inspections passed, the crew installs wooden forms inside the excavated section. These forms shape the new concrete footing. Rebar is placed inside according to the engineer's reinforcement specifications.

Then the concrete is poured. For Toronto underpinning projects, a 32 megapascal concrete mix is standard — this is the same strength concrete used in commercial construction.

After the pour, the concrete cures for a minimum of 48 hours before forms are removed. The structural engineer specifies the curing window. You cannot rush it.

---

### [5:50 — STEP 7: NON-SHRINK GROUT AND LOAD TRANSFER]

Once the forms come off, a layer of non-shrink grout is packed between the new concrete footing and the underside of the existing foundation wall above it. This fills the gap, creates a solid continuous connection, and transfers the load from the house structure into the new footing.

This step is what actually does the work. The grout is what connects old to new.

After section one is complete and the load is transferred, the process moves to the next section in the engineered sequence. Demolition, excavation, inspection, forming, pour, cure, grout. Repeat for every section around the perimeter.

---

### [6:30 — STEP 8: WATERPROOFING AND DRAINAGE SYSTEM]

Once the underpinning is complete, the basement perimeter is open — and this is the right time to install the interior waterproofing system.

A drainage channel is cut along the perimeter and a perforated pipe is installed in a gravel bed. A dimple membrane goes on the foundation walls from top to bottom, directing any water that seeps through the wall down into the drainage channel.

All water collected in the drainage channel flows to the sump pit.

If you're doing a combined underpinning and waterproofing project, the waterproofing installation costs $50 to $80 per linear foot because the trench is already open. Done separately afterward, the same work costs $80 to $120 per linear foot. Bundling saves you $3,000 to $6,000.

---

### [7:45 — STEP 9: SUMP PUMP INSTALLATION]

The sump pit is excavated at the lowest point in the drainage system. A liner goes in, and the primary sump pump is installed. When groundwater rises to the trigger point, the pump automatically activates and discharges the water outside through an ABS pipe.

For finished basements or homes in lower-lying Toronto neighbourhoods, we strongly recommend adding a battery backup pump. If the power goes out during a storm, the battery backup takes over. That's the scenario where basements flood.

---

### [8:30 — STEP 10: NEW CONCRETE FLOOR PREPARATION]

With waterproofing and drainage complete, the plumbing inspection is scheduled. Once it passes, the new slab preparation begins.

Four to five inches of clean gravel is spread across the entire floor area and compacted with a vibratory plate. This is the stable base.

Over the gravel goes a poly moisture barrier — this blocks ground moisture from migrating up into the slab and the basement air.

On top of that, we install two inches of rigid foam insulation. R10 thermal layer. This separates the slab from the cold ground and keeps your basement floor warm. It pays back over time in lower heating bills.

Finally, welded steel wire mesh goes in to reinforce the concrete and control cracking.

---

### [9:30 — STEP 11: CONCRETE FLOOR POUR AND FINISH]

The new slab is poured using the same 32 megapascal mix as the footings. Typically three to four inches thick. After placement, the surface is levelled, troweled, and finished smooth — ready for flooring, tile, or a polished concrete finish.

---

### [10:15 — STEP 12: FINAL INSPECTION AND PERMIT CLOSE-OUT]

The structural engineer returns for the final inspection. They review the completed structure, confirm all work matches the approved drawings, and issue the closing report for the City of Toronto building inspector.

The City inspector does the final sign-off. Once that passes, the permit is closed in the City's system.

Keep the permit, all inspection records, and the close-out letter. You'll need them when you sell the home, and your insurer may request them if you ever make a structural claim.
