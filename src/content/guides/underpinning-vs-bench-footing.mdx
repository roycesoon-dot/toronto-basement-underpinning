Toronto homeowners planning to lower their basement face a choice that few contractors explain honestly: full underpinning or bench footing. Both gain ceiling height. Both require permits and structural engineering. The costs and tradeoffs are meaningfully different.

We give every client a straight comparison before they sign anything. Here is the same breakdown we use on-site.

## What Each Method Does

**Full underpinning** extends the existing foundation footings deeper into the ground by excavating and pouring new mass-concrete sections in a staged sequence beneath the original footings. Once the new footings cure, the basement floor slab is dug out and re-poured at the new lower elevation. The result: full ceiling height from wall to wall, with no reduction in floor area.

**Bench footing** builds a new concrete "bench" against the inside face of the existing foundation walls instead of digging underneath them. The bench sits at the original footing level and provides bearing support. The floor slab is then lowered in the central area only. The perimeter bench remains at the original floor level, creating a step or ledge around the edge of the basement.

## When Bench Footing Makes Sense

Bench footing is a legitimate and safe option in specific situations. We recommend it when:

*   **Budget is the primary constraint** and the homeowner accepts reduced floor area
*   **The basement will remain unfinished** — a utility or storage space where the bench is not a functional problem
*   **Soil conditions make under-footing excavation unusually complex** — high water table, loose fill, or proximity to deep utility lines
*   **Timelines are extremely tight** — benching can be completed faster than full underpinning because the staged pin-section sequence is eliminated

We do not recommend bench footing if a legal basement apartment is the goal, if the home is semi-detached (the bench eats into the already narrower floor plan), or if the homeowner plans to sell within ten years and wants maximum resale value.

## When Full Underpinning Is the Right Call

Full underpinning delivers the better long-term return in virtually every Toronto context where floor space matters. The scenarios where we consistently recommend full underpinning:

*   **Legal secondary suite creation** — Ontario Building Code Section 9.36 requires minimum floor area and ceiling height throughout. A perimeter bench complicates compliance.
*   **Semi-detached and row homes** — the floor plan is already narrow. Losing 18-24 inches on each wall turns a functional apartment into a cramped one.
*   **High-end renovations** — Forest Hill, Rosedale, Lawrence Park clients paying for full basement finishing do not want a visible concrete ledge around the perimeter.
*   **Long-term holds** — if you plan to hold the property for 10+ years, the additional resale premium from full-height, edge-to-edge space pays back the cost difference.

  In Toronto's current market, a finished legal basement apartment generates $1,800–$2,400 per month in rental income. Full underpinning pays for itself in 18–36 months at those rental rates. Bench footing, if it limits legal suite eligibility, generates zero rental income.

## The Cost Gap Is Narrower Than It Looks

Bench footing appears cheaper until you account for what you are buying. A $40,000 bench footing that reduces usable floor area by 90 square feet in a Toronto basement is more expensive per usable square foot than a $60,000 full underpinning project that preserves every inch.
