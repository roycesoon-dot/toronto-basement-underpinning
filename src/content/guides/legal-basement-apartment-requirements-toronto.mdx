Creating a legal basement apartment in Toronto requires meeting a specific set of requirements across building code, fire code, and municipal registration. Here is the complete checklist — organized by category.

## Zoning and Planning

Before spending any money on construction:

*   Confirm your property is zoned to permit a secondary suite (most Toronto residential zones are, but verify at [toronto.ca/zoning](https://www.toronto.ca))
*   Check whether the suite was previously registered — if someone built a basement apartment before you owned the home, there may be outstanding orders or a prior registration to resolve
*   If the property is in an area with heritage designation, additional review may apply

## Structural and Ceiling Height

*   **Finished ceiling height:** Minimum 1.95 m (6'5") over at least 75% of required floor area
*   **No point lower than:** 1.8 m (5'11") in any habitable area
*   **Foundation integrity:** The structural engineer's assessment as part of any underpinning scope confirms the foundation is sound

For most Toronto homes built before 1960, meeting the ceiling height requirement requires basement underpinning. See [underpinning for legal basement apartments](/guide/underpinning-legal-basement-apartment-toronto/) for the full scope and ROI analysis.

## Separate Entrance

*   A separate entrance independent of the main dwelling is required
*   Entrance must provide a direct exit to the exterior without passing through the main dwelling's living space
*   Minimum clear opening: 860 mm wide, 1.98 m high
*   The entrance must be weatherproofed and have appropriate lighting

Common entrance configurations: side door, rear entrance, dedicated stairwell with exterior access at grade.

## Egress Windows in Sleeping Rooms

Each bedroom in the secondary suite requires a compliant egress window:

*   **Minimum openable area:** 0.35 m²
*   **Minimum clear opening height:** 380 mm
*   **Minimum clear opening width:** 380 mm
*   **Maximum sill height above finished floor:** 1,000 mm (allows occupant to climb out)
*   **Window well required** if below grade, with minimum dimensions to allow emergency exit

Most original basement windows in Toronto homes are too small or too high to meet egress requirements. Window replacement or new openings are part of most legal suite conversions.

<Callout type="info" title="The Egress Window Is Non-Negotiable">
  City of Toronto inspectors check egress windows carefully. A bedroom without a compliant egress window cannot be approved as a sleeping room. If your suite relies on a non-compliant window for a bedroom, you may be required to reclassify it as a den — which affects how you market and rent the suite.
</Callout>

## Fire Safety

**Fire separation between the suite and the main dwelling:**
*   Minimum 30-minute fire-rated assembly on walls and ceiling separating the suite from the main dwelling
*   Typically achieved with Type X drywall (5/8") on both sides of the shared assembly

**Smoke and CO alarms:**
*   Interconnected smoke alarms on every floor including basement, and in every bedroom
*   CO detectors as required by the Ontario Fire Code
*   All alarms must be interconnected so activation of one activates all

**Sprinkler system:**
*   Required in some configurations — your permit drawings will specify if a sprinkler is required based on suite location, floor area, and access

## Plumbing and Mechanical

*   Full bathroom with toilet, sink, and shower or tub
*   Kitchen facilities (at minimum a sink and cooking appliance; a proper kitchen improves rentability)
*   Dedicated ventilation: bathroom exhaust fan ducted to exterior, kitchen range hood or ventilation
*   Heating system adequately sized for the additional suite — either extended from the main system with sufficient capacity, or a separate system

## Electrical

*   Dedicated electrical subpanel for the suite (strongly recommended to enable separate hydro metering)
*   All outlets, switches, and fixtures to current ESA standards
*   GFCI protection in bathroom and kitchen
*   ESA permit and inspection for all electrical work

## City of Toronto Registration

After construction is complete and all inspections are passed:

1.  Submit a secondary suite registration application to the City of Toronto
2.  City schedules a final inspection
3.  Registration is issued — the unit is now a legal secondary suite on City records
4.  You may now legally rent the suite with full landlord protection under the Residential Tenancies Act

Registration also qualifies the property for the Toronto Basement Flooding Protection Subsidy if eligible equipment is installed.
