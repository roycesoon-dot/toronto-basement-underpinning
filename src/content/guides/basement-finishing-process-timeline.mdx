Basement finishing involves multiple trades working in a specific sequence. Skipping or rushing a phase creates problems that are expensive to fix after the subsequent phase is complete. Here is the correct order and what each phase involves.

## Phase 1: Design and Permits (2–6 Weeks Before Construction)

Before any physical work begins, the design is finalized and building permits are submitted to the City of Toronto.

The permit drawings typically include:
*   Floor plan with room dimensions and intended use
*   Ceiling height documentation (must demonstrate OBC 9.5.3.1 compliance)
*   Bathroom location and plumbing drain routing
*   Egress window sizes and locations (if a secondary suite is intended)
*   Electrical panel capacity confirmation

City of Toronto permit review takes 2 to 6 weeks for residential basement finishing. Expedited review is not available for most residential projects. Do not schedule trades to start until the permit is in hand.

## Phase 2: Framing (Days 1–5)

Steel stud or wood stud walls are framed out from the concrete floor:

*   Perimeter walls set off the foundation walls (leaving a gap for vapour control)
*   Interior partition walls defining rooms, bathroom, storage, and mechanical areas
*   Ceiling soffit framing to box in ductwork and beams
*   Egress window rough openings confirmed

Framing takes 3 to 5 days for a typical 700–900 square foot basement. The entire layout of the basement is established at this phase — changes after rough-in is complete are expensive.

## Phase 3: Rough-In (Days 5–15)

Three trades work in sequence through the rough-in phase:

**Plumbing rough-in (2–3 days):** New drain lines and water supply lines are installed for the bathroom and any kitchenette. Floor drain rough-ins are core-drilled through the existing slab. This must happen before insulation or drywall in floor cavities.

**Electrical rough-in (2–3 days):** New circuits are run for the basement: lighting, outlets, bathroom GFCI circuits, range circuit if a kitchenette is included, and any dedicated circuits for mechanicals. The panel may need upgrading if capacity is insufficient — this is assessed before permits are submitted.

**HVAC rough-in (1–2 days):** Supply and return ductwork extended to serve the basement living spaces. New diffusers located for each room. Bathroom exhaust fan ducted to exterior.

City inspection is required after rough-in is complete and before insulation is installed — the inspector must be able to see the framing, plumbing, and electrical before the walls are closed.

<Callout type="info" title="The Inspection Hold">
  The rough-in inspection creates a mandatory pause in the project. Schedule it as early as possible — inspection slots can take 3 to 7 business days to book. Experienced contractors build this delay into their schedule rather than treating it as a surprise.
</Callout>

## Phase 4: Insulation (Days 15–18)

After rough-in inspection sign-off:

*   Perimeter walls insulated between studs (batt insulation or spray foam in the stud cavity)
*   Vapour barrier installed on the warm side of the insulation (interior face)
*   Ceiling insulation where required for sound attenuation between floors

## Phase 5: Drywall (Days 18–25)

Drywall installation runs in three sub-phases:
1.  Hang (all sheets fastened to studs and ceiling)
2.  Tape (all joints and corners filled and taped)
3.  Mud and sand (typically two to three coats with sanding between)

Drywall work in a 700–900 square foot basement typically takes 5 to 7 working days including all coats and sanding. The final sand generates significant dust — air filtration is important during this phase.

## Phase 6: Flooring (Days 25–30)

After drywall prime coat:
*   Flooring underlayment installed
*   LVP, laminate, tile, or carpet installed per the design specification
*   Bathroom tile and shower pan installed in the bathroom

## Phase 7: Fixtures, Trim, and Paint (Days 30–40)

*   Doors hung and trimmed
*   Baseboard and casing installed
*   Plumbing fixtures set (toilet, sink, shower fixtures)
*   Electrical trim-out (outlets, switches, light fixtures)
*   Painting — walls and ceiling
*   Cabinet installation if kitchen or wet bar is included

## Phase 8: Final Inspection and Occupancy

Final City of Toronto inspection confirms the completed work matches the permitted drawings. On approval, an occupancy certificate (or equivalent sign-off) is issued.

If the basement is a secondary suite, it must also be registered with the City of Toronto before tenants move in.

Typical full project timeline from permit approval to final inspection: **8 to 14 weeks**.
