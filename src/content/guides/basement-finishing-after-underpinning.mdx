The underpinning crew has packed up. The new slab has been poured. Now what? Planning the transition from structural work to finished basement carefully saves time, money, and avoids problems that show up after the walls are closed.

## The Minimum Wait Before Finishing Begins

**Concrete curing time:** The new slab poured after underpinning reaches sufficient strength for light foot traffic within 48 to 72 hours. Structural loads (framing, materials staging) can be placed on the slab after 7 days. Full design strength (28-day cure) should be reached before any heavy point loads are applied.

In practice, the limiting factor is not the slab — it is the structural engineer's final sign-off.

**Structural engineer sign-off:** Before the finishing permit is submitted or framing begins, the structural engineer who supervised the underpinning must provide a letter or stamped document confirming the underpinning work was completed per the approved drawings. This is typically issued within 1 to 2 weeks of the final pour, after the engineer's final site inspection.

**Moisture monitoring:** New concrete off-gasses moisture as it cures. Before any vapour barrier or flooring is installed, the slab moisture content should be measured. Most installers require a concrete moisture vapour emission rate (MVER) below 3 lbs/1000 sq ft/24 hours for standard flooring. LVP and tile are more tolerant; engineered hardwood requires lower moisture levels.

## Layout Decisions Made Better After Underpinning

Underpinning creates height that did not exist before. The new dimensions change what is possible and what makes sense. Plan the finishing layout after the structural work is complete — not before — because:

**Mechanical relocation becomes viable.** With the basement floor now 12 to 18 inches lower, duct runs and pipes that were ceiling-height obstructions are now at shoulder or head height. A mechanical contractor can often reroute these to reclaim clearance in specific areas. This may eliminate the need for a dropped soffit that was previously unavoidable.

**Bathroom placement.** New bathroom drains require core drilling through the new slab. Plan the bathroom location and route drains before the new slab is poured if possible — or at minimum before the finishing permit is submitted. Changing a bathroom location after a slab is poured requires cutting through new concrete.

**Egress window upgrades.** If the increased ceiling height enables a legal secondary suite that was not previously feasible, the suite's egress window requirements kick in. Window upgrades should be planned and permitted concurrently with the finishing permit.

<Callout type="tip" title="Plan Finishing Before Underpinning Starts">
  Even though finishing cannot begin until after underpinning, planning the finishing layout before underpinning starts pays off. The underpinning crew can core-drill plumbing rough-ins and rough-in window openings during the structural phase, while the slab is still being poured. Doing this after the fact requires cutting a new slab.
</Callout>

## The Turnkey Advantage

The most efficient path from bare slab to finished basement suite is a single turnkey contract covering underpinning, waterproofing, and finishing. The advantages:

**One project manager:** No coordination overhead between three separate contractors. The project manager knows the underpinning was done to spec, plans the finishing layout accordingly, and schedules trades in the correct sequence.

**Concurrent permits:** A single contractor can submit the underpinning permit and the finishing permit concurrently, reducing the overall permit wait. Two separate contractors submit separately, with the finishing permit delayed until the structural sign-off is complete.

**Single warranty:** One warranty covers the structural work, the waterproofing, and the finished suite. If water appears in the finished basement, there is no dispute about which contractor is responsible.

**Cost savings:** Shared mobilization, shared skip bin rental, and reduced site management overhead make a turnkey contract 5 to 12 percent less expensive than three separate contracts for the same scope.
