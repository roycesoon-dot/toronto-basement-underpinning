The two most common responses to foundation cracking in Toronto are crack injection and basement underpinning. They address fundamentally different problems, at costs an order of magnitude apart. Getting the diagnosis right matters.

## What Each Approach Fixes

**Polyurethane or epoxy crack injection** seals an existing crack in a poured concrete or masonry wall. Injection ports are drilled into the crack at intervals, and a flexible polyurethane resin (or rigid epoxy for structural repairs) is injected under pressure to fill the void from the inside face to the exterior face of the wall.

Crack injection fixes: **water entry through an existing crack in an otherwise sound foundation.** It does not fix the underlying cause of cracking — it seals the symptom.

**Basement underpinning** extends the foundation footings deeper into the ground. It addresses settlement or inadequate bearing capacity at the footing level — the root cause of most structural foundation cracking.

Underpinning fixes: **footing that is too shallow, settling in poor soil, or frost-affected.** It does not fill cracks — it eliminates the cause of ongoing crack propagation.

## The Diagnostic Decision Tree

**Step 1: Is the crack active or stable?**

A crack is stable if its width and length have not changed in 12+ months. A crack is active if it is growing wider, longer, or has displaced (one face set back from the other).

*   **Stable crack → crack injection is likely appropriate.** The movement has stopped; sealing against water entry is the goal.
*   **Active crack → determine the cause before any repair.** Injecting an active crack is temporary at best.

**Step 2: What type of crack is it?**

*   **Vertical shrinkage cracks in poured concrete:** Almost always suitable for injection. The wall is structurally sound; the crack is a curing artifact or minor movement event.
*   **Horizontal cracks in block or brick walls:** The wall is being pushed inward by lateral soil pressure. Crack injection does nothing for wall movement. Structural reinforcement (steel ties, wall anchors, or full replacement) or underpinning to relieve pressure may be needed.
*   **Stair-step cracks with displacement:** Indicates differential settlement. The cause of the differential settlement must be identified before repair. If one footing section is failing while others are stable, underpinning that specific section may be needed.

**Step 3: Is there evidence of active settlement?**

Signs that the footing itself is failing, not just the wall:
*   The crack is consistently wider at the top than the bottom (or vice versa)
*   Adjacent doors or windows are sticking or have visibly racked
*   The wall has a visible bow or lean inward
*   Multiple cracks exist in a concentrated area
*   The crack appeared after a specific event (very cold winter, saturated soil season)

Active settlement typically requires underpinning of the affected section(s), not crack injection.

<Callout type="info" title="The Engineer's Role">
  For any crack showing displacement, horizontal orientation, or evidence of active movement, a structural engineer assessment comes before any contractor work. The engineer identifies the cause and specifies the repair. A contractor who recommends underpinning for a stable vertical crack — or injection for a horizontally-cracking block wall — should be questioned.
</Callout>

## Cost Comparison

| Repair Type | Typical Toronto Cost | What It Solves |
|---|---|---|
| Polyurethane injection (single crack) | $500–$1,500 | Water entry through stable crack |
| Epoxy injection (structural crack) | $800–$2,500 | Structural integrity + water entry |
| Wall anchor system (horizontal cracks) | $4,000–$10,000 | Lateral wall movement |
| Partial underpinning (one section) | $8,000–$20,000 | Localized footing failure |
| Full perimeter underpinning | $25,000–$80,000+ | Full footing extension |

## The Right Approach for Common Toronto Scenarios

**Pre-war brick semi-detached home, vertical crack at the corner, stable for 3 years:** → Crack injection. Stable, known cause, water entry the only concern.

**1950s North York bungalow, horizontal crack mid-height on block wall, wall bowing 10 mm:** → Structural engineer assessment, likely wall anchors or wall replacement. Injection will not hold.

**Annex Victorian, multiple stair-step cracks with displacement, doors sticking upstairs:** → Structural engineer assessment, likely partial underpinning on the settling section.

**Scarborough detached, single vertical crack after last winter, no displacement, dry otherwise:** → Monitor for one season, inject if it does not grow.
