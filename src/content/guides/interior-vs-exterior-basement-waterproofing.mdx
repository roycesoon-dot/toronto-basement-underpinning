Homeowners researching basement waterproofing will encounter two fundamentally different approaches. Understanding which one makes sense for a Toronto home — and why — requires knowing what each method actually does.

## What Each Method Does

**Interior waterproofing** installs a drainage system inside the foundation perimeter. Water that penetrates the foundation wall or enters through the footing-wall joint is captured by a perimeter drainage channel (weeping tile), redirected to a sump pit, and pumped out. The water still enters the wall — the system controls where it goes before it can cause damage.

**Exterior waterproofing** excavates around the outside of the foundation to expose the full wall face. A waterproof membrane is applied directly to the exterior wall surface, and drainage board and weeping tile are installed in the backfill zone. The goal is to prevent water from ever contacting the wall face in the first place.

## Why Interior Waterproofing Dominates Toronto Residential Projects

The practical reality for most Toronto homeowners:

**Semi-detached and row homes cannot be exterior-waterproofed on the shared wall.** There is no access to excavate along the party wall without trespassing on and disturbing the neighbour's foundation. For the party wall side — which is often the wettest side of the basement because water naturally drains between two structures — interior waterproofing is the only feasible option.

**Exterior excavation destroys landscaping.** For Toronto homes with mature trees, fences, patios, driveways, or gardens adjacent to the foundation, exterior excavation means losing all of it. Many homeowners are not willing to accept that disruption even when cost is not the deciding factor.

**Interior waterproofing handles hydrostatic pressure more reliably.** Toronto's clay soils create sustained hydrostatic pressure on foundation walls — it is not just surface water, it is groundwater maintained by clay's low permeability. An interior drainage system that actively pumps water away from the perimeter is more reliable for managing this condition than an exterior membrane that must resist constant pressure without active drainage.

<Callout type="tip" title="When to Consider Exterior Waterproofing">
  Exterior waterproofing makes practical sense in two Toronto scenarios: (1) new construction or major additions where the exterior is already being excavated, and (2) detached homes with significant parging deterioration on the exterior wall face that is causing wall degradation, not just water entry. In these cases, addressing the exterior wall surface directly is warranted.
</Callout>

## The Bottom Line

For most Toronto semi-detached and row homes, interior waterproofing is the right solution. It is effective, practical, significantly less disruptive, and substantially less expensive than exterior waterproofing.

For detached homes with accessible perimeters and moderate water problems, exterior waterproofing is worth getting a quote for comparison — but the cost premium is substantial.
