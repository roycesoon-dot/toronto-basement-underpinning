Homeowners who know exactly what to expect before construction starts have a much easier time living through it. Here is an honest, phase-by-phase account of what basement underpinning construction looks like from the day the crew arrives to the day they leave.

## Week 1–2: Site Preparation and Mobilization

**Day 1:** The crew arrives to set up site protection. This includes:

*   Dust isolation walls at the top of the basement stairs and around any basement-to-main-floor openings
*   Floor protection on the main floor path to the basement access point
*   Skip bin placement on the driveway or street (permit required from the City of Toronto for street placement)
*   Initial equipment delivery — mini-excavator for larger basements, hand tools for tight access sites

**Days 2–5:** The existing basement slab is saw-cut along the perimeter and broken up with a jackhammer. This is the loudest phase of the project. Expect significant noise for 2 to 4 days. The broken concrete is wheelbarrowed out and loaded into the skip bin.

## Weeks 2–6: Pin Excavation and Pouring

This is the core of the structural work and the phase that takes the longest. The crew works through the perimeter in a carefully engineered sequence.

**Each pin section takes approximately 2 to 4 days:**

1.  Excavate the pin section to the engineered footing depth
2.  Form the new footing using timber forms or ICF
3.  Pour mass concrete (minimum 25 MPa)
4.  Allow curing time before moving to the next pin — typically 48 to 72 hours minimum
5.  Structural engineer site visit to inspect and approve completed sections (required at intervals per the permit)

**What daily life looks like during this phase:**
*   Crew typically on-site from 7:00 AM to 4:00 PM on weekdays
*   Moderate noise during excavation (shovels, mini-excavator if used)
*   Periodic concrete truck deliveries — typically one small truck per pin section
*   Limited access to the basement during active excavation on that day's pin
*   Upper floors fully accessible and habitable throughout

<Callout type="info" title="The Curing Window Is Not Wasted Time">
  When no crew appears on a particular day, it usually means a recently poured section is curing. This is mandatory — the concrete must reach structural strength before adjacent sections are excavated. Rushing the cure is how structural problems happen. A reputable contractor will explain exactly what phase they are in at any point.
</Callout>

## Weeks 6–8: Waterproofing (If Combined)

If interior waterproofing is part of the same project — which we strongly recommend, as the perimeter is already open — this phase follows immediately after the last pin section cures.

*   Weeping tile is laid in gravel along the footing perimeter
*   Sump pit is excavated and liner installed
*   Dimpled membrane is applied to the inside face of the foundation walls
*   Drainage system is connected to the sump pit

Adding waterproofing during underpinning saves the cost of saw-cutting and excavating the perimeter a second time in the future. The marginal cost during underpinning is a fraction of what it costs as a standalone project.

## Weeks 7–10: New Slab Pour and Backfill

Once all pin sections are poured, cured, and signed off by the structural engineer:

*   Gravel base is compacted and levelled to the new floor elevation
*   Vapour barrier is installed over the gravel
*   New concrete slab is poured — typically 4 inches thick with wire mesh or rebar
*   Slab cures for a minimum of 7 days before any traffic or finishing work begins

The basement is now at the new lowered elevation. The structural phase is complete.

## Communication During the Project

A professional contractor provides:

*   A written project schedule before work starts showing anticipated phase dates
*   Daily or weekly updates on progress, especially when curing delays push the schedule
*   Advance notice of particularly noisy or disruptive days (slab saw-cutting, jack-hammering)
*   Structural engineer reports after each inspection
*   Clear point of contact (site supervisor and office) for any questions

If a contractor goes quiet mid-project and you cannot get straight answers about the schedule, that is a red flag.
