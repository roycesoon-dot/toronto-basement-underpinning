Basement underpinning cannot begin without a set of engineered drawings that define exactly how the structural work will be done. These drawings are submitted with the building permit application and remain on-site throughout construction as the contractor's guide and the inspector's reference.

## Why Drawings Are Required

The City of Toronto requires structural drawings for all foundation alterations because:

*   Underpinning extends structural elements that the entire building depends on
*   The Ontario Building Code requires that a Professional Engineer design and supervise structural foundation work
*   Inspectors use the drawings at mandatory site inspections to verify the work matches what was approved
*   The drawings become a permanent record of what was done, available to future owners

A contractor who says you can underpin without drawings and permits is either describing illegal work or does not understand the regulatory requirements. Either way, do not hire them.

### 1. Site Plan

A scaled plan showing:
*   Property lot lines and dimensions
*   Building footprint with dimensions
*   Required setbacks from lot lines
*   Location of any accessory structures (garage, shed)
*   North arrow and scale

The site plan establishes that the project is within the zoning envelope and helps the reviewer understand the property context.

### 2. Foundation Plan

A scaled plan of the existing foundation showing:
*   Foundation wall locations and dimensions
*   Existing footing dimensions (from as-built records or on-site measurement)
*   Existing slab thickness and elevation
*   Location of all structural openings (windows, stairs, access hatches)
*   Proposed new footing extent and depth — shown overlaid on the existing foundation

### 3. Footing Section Details

Cross-section drawings showing:
*   Existing footing dimensions and depth
*   New footing extension dimensions: width, thickness, and depth
*   New footing depth relative to the frost line (minimum 1.2 m below grade in Toronto)
*   Concrete specification: minimum 25 MPa for residential underpinning footings
*   Reinforcement: rebar specification if required by the engineer
*   The mechanical key or hook connection between the new footing extension and the existing footing

These section details are what the inspector examines at the footing-before-pour inspection.

### 4. Pin Sequence Diagram

A foundation plan annotated to show:
*   Each pin section numbered in the order it is to be excavated and poured
*   Pin section dimensions (typically 3 to 4 feet wide for residential projects)
*   Maximum span between simultaneously open sections
*   Required curing time between adjacent pours

This diagram is what makes underpinning safe — it ensures no crew on any job site can skip or reorder the excavation sequence without it being visible on the approved drawings.

<Callout type="info" title="The Engineer Visits During Construction">
  The engineer who stamps the drawings is required to conduct site inspections during construction — not just sign the paper and disappear. Inspections occur at minimum at the footing-before-pour stage and at project completion. Ask your contractor when the engineer will be on-site and what they will inspect.
</Callout>

### 5. Schedule 1 (Designer Information)

Required by the Ontario Building Code Act: a signed declaration from the engineer of record confirming they designed the project and accept the professional responsibility for the structural elements.

## Who Prepares the Drawings

An Ontario Professional Engineer (P.Eng.) with structural engineering designation prepares and stamps the drawings. In some cases, an architect coordinates the project and the structural engineer provides the structural drawings as part of the architect's package.
