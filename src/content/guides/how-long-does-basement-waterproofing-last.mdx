Interior waterproofing is a permanent installation — the weeping tile, drainage membrane, and sump system are designed to outlast the home if properly maintained. Here is what drives longevity and what you should expect from a warranty.

## Component Lifespan Breakdown

An interior waterproofing system has three main components with different service lives:

**Weeping tile (perforated drainage pipe):** Modern PVC or HDPE weeping tile has an indefinite service life under normal conditions. The pipe does not degrade in soil. The primary maintenance concern is the sock filter — a geotextile sleeve wrapped around the pipe that prevents silt infiltration. In high-silt soils (common in parts of North York and Scarborough), the sock can eventually clog. Inspection via camera every 10 to 15 years is recommended if you have had issues.

**Dimpled membrane:** Delta MS-style membranes are made from high-density polyethylene and are rated for 25 to 50+ year service life in below-grade applications. The membrane is not UV-exposed and faces no mechanical stress in normal use. The weak points are the termination edges and the capping strip at the top — these should be inspected if any water migration is observed behind the membrane.

**Sump pump system:** The mechanical components have the shortest service life. A quality primary sump pump lasts 7 to 12 years. The battery in a battery backup unit needs replacement every 3 to 5 years. The discharge line and check valve should be inspected annually.

## What Affects Longevity

**Soil conditions:** Toronto's heavy clay soils retain water and maintain sustained hydrostatic pressure on foundation walls. High-clay sites put more load on the drainage system year-round. The system handles it — it was designed for this — but pump cycling frequency is higher and inspections matter more.

**Groundwater depth:** Properties near ravines, creeks, or former watercourses (common in older Toronto neighbourhoods like The Annex, Rosedale, and High Park) have higher water table exposure. The sump pump runs more frequently, and pump replacement cycles are shorter.

**Sewer backup vs. groundwater:** Interior waterproofing manages groundwater entry. It does not protect against municipal sewer backup, which requires a separate backwater valve. Make sure you know which problem you have — or have both solutions installed if both are risks in your area.

**Installation quality:** The most significant factor in long-term performance is whether the original installation was done correctly. Specifically: adequate slope toward the sump pit, properly lapped membrane terminations, and a correctly sized pump for the water volume. A system installed by an experienced crew performing dozens of Toronto jobs per year outperforms a one-off installation by a general contractor.

<Callout type="info" title="Annual Maintenance Checklist">
  Test your sump pump every spring before the thaw by pouring water into the pit until it activates. Check the discharge line is clear and drains away from the foundation. Test the battery backup by unplugging the primary pump. Clean the intake screen if accessible. This 20-minute annual check prevents the vast majority of pump failures.
</Callout>

## What a Proper Warranty Should Cover

Reputable Toronto waterproofing contractors offer transferable warranties that cover:

*   **Water entry** through the waterproofed perimeter — if water comes in through a warrantied section, the contractor returns and resolves it at no cost
*   **System components** — typically a workmanship warranty of 10 to 25 years on the drainage installation itself
*   **Transferability** — a transferable warranty adds value to the property at resale. Ask explicitly whether the warranty transfers to a new owner.

What warranties typically do not cover:
*   Sump pump failure (pump is mechanical, has its own manufacturer warranty)
*   Sewer backup (separate coverage, requires backwater valve)
*   Wall movement or new structural cracks that create new water entry paths
