A primary sump pump that runs on household current has one critical vulnerability: power outages. Toronto's worst flooding events — the spring thaw, summer thunderstorms — coincide with power interruptions. A battery backup pump eliminates that vulnerability entirely.

## What a Battery Backup Pump Does

A battery backup sump pump is a secondary pump installed in the same pit as the primary pump, powered by a dedicated battery rather than household current. It activates automatically when:

*   **The primary pump loses power** (circuit breaker trips, outlet fails, power outage)
*   **The primary pump fails mechanically** (motor seized, float switch stuck, impeller blocked)
*   **The primary pump is overwhelmed** by incoming water volume and the pit level rises above the backup's float trigger

The backup pump discharges through its own discharge line (or through the same line via a check valve configuration) and continues pumping until either power is restored, the primary pump resumes operation, or the battery is depleted.

## Types of Backup Pump Systems

**12V DC Battery Backup:** The most common residential system. A 12-volt deep-cycle or AGM battery powers a DC pump motor. The battery is continuously trickle-charged from household current so it remains at full charge until needed. Typical run time: 5 to 8 hours continuous, 1 to 3 days intermittent. Battery replacement every 3 to 5 years.

**AC/DC Combo (Dual-Mode):** More advanced units run on AC power when available (using house current as primary power for the backup pump) and automatically switch to battery when the power is out. These units can run on battery longer because they were not running on battery during normal operation.

**High-Capacity Systems:** For basements with high water tables, near ravines, or with large drainage areas, high-capacity backup systems use larger batteries (marine deep-cycle or sealed lead-acid) and higher-flow pumps. These are specified based on your drainage system's flow requirements.

## Battery Types: What to Know

**AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat):** The standard recommendation for sump backup systems. Maintenance-free, sealed, no off-gassing, tolerates partial discharge cycles well. Service life 3 to 6 years. 

**Wet-Cell Lead Acid:** Less expensive but requires periodic water level checks and produces hydrogen gas when charging — requires ventilation. Not recommended for enclosed pit installations.

**Lithium (emerging):** Some newer systems use lithium batteries for higher energy density and longer life. More expensive upfront but 8 to 12 year service life.

<Callout type="info" title="Test Your Backup Every Spring">
  Before the spring thaw: unplug the primary pump from the outlet. Pour water into the pit until the backup's float triggers. Confirm the backup activates and pumps down the pit. Re-plug the primary pump. This 5-minute test catches failed backup batteries before they are needed.
</Callout>

## Why Toronto Basements Specifically Need Backup

Toronto's flooding risk profile makes backup essential:

**Storm and thaw coincide with outages.** The ice storms and heavy rainfall events that cause the highest water table conditions in Toronto also knock out power. During the July 2013 Toronto flood — the costliest weather event in Ontario history — tens of thousands of basements flooded when pumps lost power or were overwhelmed.

**Heavy clay soils maintain hydrostatic pressure.** Even after rain stops, Toronto's clay soils continue releasing stored water into drainage systems for days. A power outage that lasts 12 to 24 hours exposes basements to sustained pressure without a functioning pump.

**Insurance does not fully cover preventable flooding.** Most Toronto home insurance policies cover sewer backup with a rider, but claims related to groundwater entry through failed drainage systems may be limited. A $1,200 backup system prevents a $30,000 to $80,000 flood claim.
