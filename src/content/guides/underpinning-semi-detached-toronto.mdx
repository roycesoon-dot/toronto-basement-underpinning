More than half of Toronto's pre-war housing stock is semi-detached or in a row formation. These homes dominate neighbourhoods like Leslieville, Little Italy, The Junction, Roncesvalles, and Cabbagetown — and they are some of the most common candidates for basement underpinning.

A shared wall adds considerations that detached homes do not have. Here is how experienced Toronto contractors handle them.

## Why Semis Are Excellent Underpinning Candidates

Toronto's semi-detached stock was built predominantly between 1880 and 1960. Original basement ceiling heights in these homes range from 6'0" to 6'8" — below the Ontario Building Code 9.36 minimum for a finished legal space.

The homes are narrow (typically 14 to 18 feet wide), which means every square foot of basement space matters. Full underpinning — not bench footing — is almost always the right approach because losing floor area to a perimeter bench in a narrow semi creates a space that is barely usable.

The good news: semi-detached underpinning is standard practice in Toronto. Every qualified contractor has done hundreds of them.

## The Shared Wall: What Actually Needs to Happen

The critical question on any semi-detached underpin is the shared (party) wall. There are two scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Your half has its own independent foundation footing.** This is common in homes built after roughly 1920. Your footing runs along your side of the party wall but does not extend under your neighbour's side. Underpinning can proceed entirely on your property without structurally involving your neighbour's home.

**Scenario 2: A shared or continuous footing runs under the party wall.** This is common in older Victorian and Edwardian semis. Excavating too close to the shared footing without engineering guidance can affect the neighbour's bearing capacity.

The structural engineer's assessment resolves which scenario applies to your specific home. This is done at the pre-construction stage, before permits are submitted.

## Party Wall Agreements

A Party Wall Agreement (PWA) is a written document between two adjoining property owners that:

*   Confirms notification of intended construction
*   Documents the current condition of the shared wall (pre-construction survey with photos)
*   Establishes protocols if any damage occurs during the work
*   Specifies access rights if the contractor needs to work from the neighbour's side

PWAs are not legally mandated in Ontario the way they are in some jurisdictions, but most structural engineers require them when a shared footing is involved. A reputable Toronto contractor will draft and coordinate the agreement as part of their pre-construction process.

<Callout type="info" title="Pre-Construction Survey">
  Before any digging starts, document the existing condition of your shared wall — cracks, movement, water damage. Photos with timestamps protect both you and your neighbour if any dispute arises later. Most professional contractors do this automatically.
</Callout>

## Sequencing on a Shared Wall

When the underpinning sequence reaches the wall that runs along the party line, the pin sections are typically made smaller — sometimes 2 feet wide instead of the standard 3 to 4 feet. This reduces the unsupported span adjacent to the shared structure.

The engineer's drawings specify the sequence and pin sizing for the party wall sections specifically. Following that sequence is mandatory, not optional.

## What Your Neighbour Needs to Know

We recommend a face-to-face conversation with your neighbour before the contractor even arrives — not just a legal notice. Explain:

*   What the project involves
*   Approximate timeline (most semi underpins run 4 to 8 weeks for the structural phase)
*   That there will be noise, a skip bin, and contractor vehicles on the street
*   That a pre-construction survey will document the current wall condition

Neighbours who feel informed are far less likely to raise objections mid-project. Neighbours who are surprised by excavation equipment showing up next door create delays and disputes.

## Accessing Your Basement: Narrow Lot Logistics

Semi-detached homes often have limited side-yard access — sometimes as narrow as 24 inches. Material staging, equipment access, and spoil removal all need to account for this constraint.

Experienced contractors use smaller excavation equipment (mini-excavators or hand excavation where necessary) and schedule material deliveries and spoil removal to minimize street presence. If your only access is through the front door or a narrow side gate, make sure your contractor has handled this before — it requires specific equipment and planning.
