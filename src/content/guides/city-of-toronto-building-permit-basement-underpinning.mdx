The building permit for basement underpinning is not optional and is not a formality. The City of Toronto uses the permit process to verify that the structural engineering is sound before any excavation begins. Here is exactly how it works.

## Why a Permit Is Required

Basement underpinning involves modifying a building's structural foundation. Ontario Building Code Part 9 and specifically Section 9.13 govern these structural alterations. The City of Toronto requires a building permit to:

*   Confirm that an Ontario-licensed structural engineer has designed the footing extension
*   Verify the construction methods comply with OBC structural requirements
*   Schedule mandatory inspections at key stages during construction
*   Maintain a record of the permitted work for future owners and insurance purposes

Starting underpinning work without a permit is an offence under the Building Code Act and exposes the homeowner to stop-work orders, fines, and the requirement to open up completed work for inspection.

## Phase 1: Pre-Application (4–8 Weeks Before Submission)

Before the permit application is submitted, the structural engineer must:

*   Conduct an on-site assessment of the existing foundation (soil conditions, footing depth, wall construction)
*   Confirm the project scope (depth of extension, perimeter length, new footing dimensions)
*   Prepare stamped structural drawings including: site plan, foundation plan, footing section details, and pin-section sequence diagram

If the project involves changes to the building footprint or creates new legal dwelling units, additional zoning review may be required before structural drawings are finalized.

## Phase 2: Permit Application Submission

The application package submitted to the City of Toronto Building Division includes:

*   Completed building permit application form
*   Schedule 1 (designer information) signed by the structural engineer
*   Architectural and structural drawings (stamped and signed by the engineer of record)
*   Site plan showing the building footprint, lot lines, and setbacks
*   Project value declaration (used to calculate permit fees)

Applications are submitted online through the City of Toronto's Toronto Building portal. A complete submission is critical — incomplete applications are returned for corrections, adding weeks to the timeline.

## Phase 3: Plan Review (6–12 Weeks)

City of Toronto plan reviewers examine the submitted drawings for compliance with the Ontario Building Code. For residential structural work, the review queue typically runs 6 to 12 weeks.

During this phase, the plan reviewer may issue a Deficiency List requesting clarification or revision of specific drawing elements. The engineer responds to deficiencies and resubmits. Each round of deficiency-response adds time to the review.

Experienced engineers who work regularly with the City of Toronto submit drawings that anticipate common reviewer concerns, reducing deficiency rounds.

<Callout type="info" title="Do Not Start Work During Plan Review">
  Construction cannot begin until the permit is issued and posted on-site. A common mistake is scheduling the crew to start before permit approval, then discovering the permit took longer than expected. Build the permit timeline into your project schedule from day one.
</Callout>

## Phase 4: Permit Issuance

Once the plan review is complete and all deficiencies resolved, the City issues the building permit. The permit card must be posted on-site throughout construction.

The permit specifies the mandatory inspection stages for the project.

## Phase 5: Construction and Mandatory Inspections

City of Toronto building inspections are required at specific stages during underpinning:

*   **Footings before pour:** The inspector must approve the excavated footing sections before concrete is placed. This is the most time-sensitive inspection — the forms are ready, the trench is open, and the crew is waiting.
*   **Framing/structural elements** (if the scope includes interior structural modifications)
*   **Final inspection:** After all work is complete, a final inspection confirms the work matches the approved drawings

Schedule inspections online through the City of Toronto's eSPACE portal. Inspections are typically available within 2 to 4 business days of request. Build inspection scheduling into the construction schedule.

## Phase 6: Permit Close-Out

After final inspection sign-off, the permit is closed. A Certificate of Inspection or occupancy confirmation is issued.

For secondary suites, a separate suite registration step follows permit close-out.
