A failed sump pump during Toronto's spring thaw or a summer thunderstorm is the leading cause of basement flooding. The pump does not fail without warning — here are the signals to watch for before your next wet season.

## 1. Age Over 7–10 Years

The most reliable predictor of sump pump failure is age. A quality submersible pump has a service life of 7 to 12 years. If you do not know how old your pump is, check the label on the motor housing — most manufacturers stamp the manufacture date.

Pumps over 10 years old in active service should be replaced proactively, even if they appear to be working. The internal impeller, float switch, and motor windings degrade over time in ways that are not visible externally.

## 2. Unusual Noise During Operation

A healthy sump pump runs smoothly with a consistent hum. Sounds that indicate mechanical problems:

*   **Rattling or grinding:** Debris in the impeller or worn bearing — the impeller will fail next
*   **Gurgling continuously after the pump stops:** Air in the discharge line or a failed check valve — the pump is working harder than it should
*   **Loud banging on startup:** Water hammer in the discharge pipe — indicates a check valve issue
*   **High-pitched whining:** Motor bearing failure — replacement is imminent

## 3. Running Constantly or Short-Cycling

**Constant running** during wet periods indicates the pump cannot keep up with incoming water. This may mean the pump is undersized, the impeller is partially blocked, or the weeping tile system is delivering more water than the pump can handle.

**Short-cycling** (pump turns on and off repeatedly within a few minutes) indicates a float switch malfunction. The pump turns on when it should not, runs briefly, turns off, and immediately turns on again. This pattern burns out the motor quickly.

## 4. Pump Does Not Activate When Tested

Every spring, before the melt, pour a bucket of water slowly into the sump pit until the float rises and the pump activates. If the pump does not activate:

*   Check that the pump is plugged in and the circuit is live
*   Check that the float is not stuck against the pit wall
*   If the pump receives power but does not run, the motor has seized

A pump that does not activate when manually tested cannot be relied on during a real event.

## 5. Visible Rust on the Housing or Discharge Line

Surface rust on the exterior of a submersible pump housing indicates extended exposure to standing water. While some rust on cast iron housings is normal, significant corrosion on the motor housing or discharge line connections indicates the pump has been sitting in water rather than actively pumping it — a sign the float switch is not functioning correctly.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Battery Backup Test">
  After testing the primary pump, unplug it from the outlet and pour water into the pit again. The battery backup unit should activate. If it does not — or if it runs but the battery drains in under an hour — the backup battery needs replacement. Backup batteries should be replaced every 3 to 5 years.
</Callout>

## 6. Water in the Pit Without the Pump Running

If you open the pit and find standing water above the intake level with the pump plugged in, the float switch or the pump itself has failed. This is an emergency — replace the pump before the next rain event.

## 7. The Discharge Line Is Frozen or Blocked

Toronto's winters can freeze exterior discharge lines, especially if they terminate too close to grade or discharge into a low-lying area that collects ice. A frozen discharge line backs up water into the pit until the pump overheats. Inspect the exterior discharge termination every winter.
