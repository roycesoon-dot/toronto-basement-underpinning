Not every crack in a foundation wall is a crisis. Toronto homes develop cracks for many reasons — concrete shrinkage, seasonal movement, differential settlement, and soil pressure. Knowing which category a crack falls into determines whether you need immediate repair, monitoring, or nothing at all.

## Crack Patterns and What They Mean

**Vertical cracks (top to bottom):** The most common crack type in poured concrete foundations. Narrow vertical cracks (under 3 mm) are typically caused by concrete shrinkage during curing or minor differential settlement. They are often stable and do not represent structural risk. They can, however, allow water infiltration. Vertical cracks wider than 3 mm or showing displacement (one side of the crack is set back from the other) indicate more significant movement.

**Horizontal cracks:** The highest concern crack type in any foundation wall. Horizontal cracking in a block or brick foundation wall indicates lateral soil pressure — the wall is being pushed inward. This is a structural problem, not just a cosmetic one. Horizontal cracks in poured concrete walls are less common but equally serious when they occur. Any horizontal crack warrants an immediate structural engineer assessment.

**Stair-step cracks (diagonal through mortar joints):** Common in brick and block foundations. Stair-step patterns follow the mortar joints and indicate differential settlement — one part of the foundation is settling at a different rate than the adjacent section. Minor stair-stepping in older Toronto brick foundations is common and often stable. Rapid progression, displacement, or associated cracking in the superstructure above requires assessment.

**Diagonal cracks from corners of openings:** Cracks radiating at 45 degrees from the corners of basement windows are common and typically indicate localized stress concentration rather than global settlement. These are often stable but should be monitored for progression.

**Floor slab cracks:** Hairline cracks in the basement floor slab are almost universally caused by concrete shrinkage and are cosmetic. Wide cracks (over 5 mm) with displacement or heaving indicate subgrade movement and should be assessed.

## Width: The Key Measurement

Width at the widest point determines urgency:

| Crack Width | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Under 0.3 mm (hairline) | Shrinkage crack | Monitor annually |
| 0.3 mm – 3 mm | Minor crack | Professional assessment |
| Over 3 mm | Significant crack | Repair required |
| Any width with displacement | Active movement | Immediate assessment |
| Horizontal (any width) | Structural concern | Immediate assessment |

## Signs of Active Movement

A crack that was stable last year may have become active. Signs of ongoing movement:

*   **The crack is wider at the top than the bottom** (or vice versa) — differential settlement is still occurring
*   **New cracking appears adjacent to an existing crack** — the fracture zone is propagating
*   **Doors or windows in the area above are sticking or not closing properly** — the superstructure is reacting to foundation movement
*   **The crack has displaced** — one face of the crack is higher, lower, or further back than the other

<Callout type="warning" title="Do Not Apply Paint Over Active Cracks">
  Painting or patching over an active crack hides the evidence of movement. If you are monitoring a crack, leave it visible so you can measure progression. Mark the crack ends with a pencil line and date it — if the crack extends beyond the mark, it is still moving.
</Callout>

## When You Need a Structural Engineer, Not Just a Contractor

Call a structural engineer directly (not just a repair contractor) when:

*   Any horizontal crack is found
*   Multiple cracks are present and showing displacement
*   Cracking is progressing rapidly (new width or length in under 6 months)
*   Cracking is accompanied by visible wall bow or lean
*   The crack pattern suggests more than one area of differential settlement

A structural engineer provides an independent assessment, not a sales pitch. The engineer's report also documents the condition before repair — important for insurance, resale, and future reference.
