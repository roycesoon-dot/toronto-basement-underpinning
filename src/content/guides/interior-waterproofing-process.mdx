Interior waterproofing installs a permanent drainage system inside your foundation perimeter to collect and redirect water before it can pool on your floor. It does not stop water from entering the wall — it captures it at the footing and moves it away.

Here is exactly how the installation works and how long each phase takes.

## Phase 1: Site Preparation (Day 1)

Before any cutting begins, the crew installs dust isolation walls using poly sheeting and tape at every opening between the basement and the upper floors. This prevents concrete dust from entering your living space.

Floor protection is laid on the path from the basement access to the exterior. The skip bin or wheelbarrow staging area is set up outside.

## Phase 2: Slab Saw-Cut and Break-Out (Day 1)

A concrete saw cuts a channel along the entire foundation wall perimeter, typically 12 to 18 inches from the wall face. The depth penetrates the existing slab (usually 3 to 5 inches) and stops at the granular base below.

The saw-cut concrete is broken up with a jack-hammer and removed by hand in sections. This is the noisiest part of the project — the saw and hammer work typically runs for 6 to 8 hours on day one.

The broken concrete is wheelbarrowed out and loaded into the skip bin.

## Phase 3: Trench Excavation and Preparation (Days 1–2)

Once the slab is removed, the crew excavates the granular base to expose the top of the footing. The trench depth is typically 6 to 12 inches below the existing slab bottom, depending on footing depth and water table conditions.

The trench bottom is graded to slope toward the sump pit location at a minimum of 1 percent grade. This ensures passive drainage toward the pump without relying on water pressure.

## Phase 4: Weeping Tile Installation (Days 2–3)

New perforated drainage pipe (weeping tile) is laid in the trench on a bed of clean crushed stone:

*   **Pipe specification:** 4-inch perforated PVC or corrugated HDPE, sock-wrapped to prevent silt infiltration
*   **Stone bed:** Minimum 6 inches of clean crushed stone (no fines) below and around the pipe
*   **Slope:** Graded toward the sump pit throughout
*   **Interior drainage board:** Many crews install a dimpled drainage board against the footing face to capture water seeping through the footing-wall joint and direct it into the weeping tile channel

The weeping tile terminates at the sump pit inlet.

## Phase 5: Sump Pit Installation (Days 2–3)

If no sump pit exists, one is excavated and installed during this phase. A standard residential sump pit is 18 to 24 inches in diameter and 24 to 36 inches deep. The liner (typically HDPE) is set in granular base and connected to the weeping tile inlet.

The sump pump (primary pedestal or submersible) and battery backup pump are installed. The discharge line is routed to the exterior, terminating at grade away from the foundation.

<Callout type="info" title="Battery Backup Is Not Optional in Toronto">
  The spring thaw and summer storm events that cause the most water entry in Toronto basements also cause power outages. A battery backup pump activates automatically when the primary pump loses power. Without backup, the pump fails exactly when you need it most.
</Callout>

## Phase 6: Dimpled Membrane (Days 3–4)

A dimpled air-gap membrane (Delta MS or equivalent) is fastened to the inside face of the foundation wall from footing to floor joist level. The membrane's dimpled surface creates a drainage plane between the wall and the membrane, allowing any water that penetrates the foundation wall to run down the face of the membrane and into the weeping tile trench below.

The membrane terminates at the top with a capping strip that is sealed to prevent soil gases from entering behind the membrane.

## Phase 7: Slab Restoration (Days 4–5)

New concrete is poured to fill the perimeter channel and restore the floor to its original level. The pour is typically a 4-inch slab matching the existing floor thickness, reinforced with wire mesh.

The slab must cure for a minimum of 7 days before traffic. During curing, the basement is accessible but the perimeter area should not be loaded.

## Total Timeline

| Phase | Duration |
|---|---|
| Site preparation | Half day |
| Saw-cut and break-out | 1 day |
| Trench excavation | 1 day |
| Weeping tile + stone | 1–2 days |
| Sump pit installation | 1 day |
| Dimpled membrane | 1 day |
| Slab restoration | 1 day pour + 7 days cure |
| **Total (mobilization to handover)** | **2–3 weeks** |
