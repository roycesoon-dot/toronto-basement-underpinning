The most cost-effective time to install interior waterproofing is during basement underpinning — not before, not after. Here is why, and how the two scopes integrate.

## Why the Timing Is Critical

Underpinning requires opening the basement perimeter. The crew excavates the existing slab along the foundation wall perimeter to access the footings for pin-section excavation. That trench is open throughout the structural phase.

Interior waterproofing requires exactly the same perimeter trench. The weeping tile, drainage stone, and sump pit are installed in the same channel that the underpinning crew already opened.

If you waterproof before underpinning, the new footing goes in below the existing weeping tile — the drainage system ends up sitting above the new footing level and becomes ineffective. The entire waterproofing installation would need to be demolished and redone after underpinning.

If you waterproof after underpinning as a separate project, the waterproofing contractor saw-cuts and excavates the perimeter again — the same work the underpinning crew already did.

Combining the two scopes in one project eliminates the duplicate excavation and restoration entirely.

## How the Combined Sequence Works

**Underpinning phase (weeks 1–8):**
The underpinning crew works through the perimeter pin sections. The trench is progressively excavated, pinned, and poured in the engineered sequence. The waterproofing installation does not begin until the last underpinning pin section has reached structural strength.

**Waterproofing installation (weeks 8–10):**
Once the structural engineer signs off on the completed underpinning work, the waterproofing installation begins:

1.  Weeping tile is laid in crushed stone at the base of the new footing elevation — integrated with the new footing geometry, not the original
2.  The drainage system connects to the sump pit, which may be newly installed or upgraded during this phase
3.  Dimpled membrane is fastened to the inside face of the foundation walls from new footing level to floor joist
4.  All drainage components are connected and tested before the slab is restored

**Slab restoration (week 10–11):**
A single slab pour restores the entire floor to the new lowered elevation. The weeping tile channel, drainage stone, and vapour barrier are all covered in this single pour — one restoration instead of two.

  A combined project under a single contractor means one warranty covering the structural underpinning and the waterproofing system. There is no grey area about which contractor is responsible if an issue arises later. Separate contractors mean separate warranties with potential gaps between them.

## The Cost Breakdown

Here is where the savings come from when combining vs separating:

| Cost Item | Combined Project | Separate Projects |
|---|---|---|
| Mobilization | Once | Twice |
| Slab saw-cut + break-out | Once | Twice |
| Perimeter excavation | Once (shared with underpinning) | Twice |
| Slab restoration | Once (new elevation pour) | Twice |
| Skip bin rental | One extended | Two separate |
| **Total duplication cost avoided** | — | **$8,000–$15,000** |

The waterproofing materials themselves (pipe, stone, membrane, pump) cost the same either way. The savings are entirely in the shared labour and demolition/restoration scope.

## When Waterproofing Is Not Needed With Underpinning

Not every underpinning project requires waterproofing. If your basement is currently dry, your existing weeping tile is functioning, and there is no evidence of past water entry, waterproofing may not be necessary.

We assess the existing drainage conditions at the free estimate visit. If the original weeping tile system is functional and the soil conditions do not indicate high water table risk, we will tell you that honestly and not include waterproofing in the scope recommendation.

If there is any question about long-term drainage performance, however, combining is almost always the right economic decision. The marginal cost of adding waterproofing during underpinning is far lower than the cost of a future flood or a second excavation.
