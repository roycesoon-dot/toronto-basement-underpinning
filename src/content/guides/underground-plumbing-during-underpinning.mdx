Basement underpinning requires opening the floor slab along the foundation perimeter. That same open floor gives plumbers direct access to the underground drain system — an opportunity that disappears the moment the new slab is poured.

Any underground plumbing work that will ever be needed in this basement should happen now.

## Why the Timing Is Perfect

The perimeter slab is already open during underpinning. The existing drains running along the footing line are exposed. The spoil excavation reaches down to footing depth — deeper than any drain access typically requires.

For a homeowner who needs to:
*   Replace a failing cast iron main drain
*   Install new floor drain rough-ins for a future bathroom
*   Run new supply lines to the basement level
*   Relocate the stack cleanout to an accessible location

...doing this work during underpinning means no additional slab cutting, no additional excavation, and no additional restoration. The plumber works in the same open trench the underpinning crew is using.

After the slab is poured, every one of these items requires cutting through new concrete — and charging for mobilization, saw-cutting, excavation, and slab restoration all over again.

## Main Drain Assessment and Replacement

Toronto's pre-1960 housing stock runs on cast iron main drains — many 60 to 80 years old. Cast iron drain pipe has a service life of 50 to 80 years. Signs the main drain is near the end of its useful life:

*   Slow drainage that cannot be cleared with snaking
*   Recurring blockages in the same location
*   Camera inspection showing significant interior corrosion, offset joints, or root intrusion
*   The drain has never been replaced in a pre-1960 home

During underpinning, the main drain running below the basement floor is accessible along much of its length. A camera inspection during the assessment phase identifies any failing sections. Replacement involves removing the cast iron segments and installing new PVC or ABS pipe with appropriate slope to the city connection.

Replacing the main drain as part of underpinning costs $3,000 to $8,000 incremental. Emergency replacement after a backup — which requires cutting a finished slab, excavation, and restoration — costs $8,000 to $18,000 and creates significant collateral damage to the finished basement.

## New Bathroom Rough-Ins

If a finished basement or legal secondary suite is in the plan, the bathroom location should be finalized before the new slab is poured. New drain rough-ins — toilet flange, shower drain, sink drain — are installed in the open floor at the correct locations and elevations relative to the new slab height.

The sequence during underpinning:
1.  Finalize bathroom location based on the new floor plan
2.  Plumber installs new drain rough-ins in the open trench before slab pour
3.  New slab is poured around the rough-in stubs, which project to the correct height above finished floor
4.  During finishing, the plumber sets the fixtures onto the pre-installed rough-ins

Getting the rough-ins in during underpinning eliminates core-drilling through the new slab later. It also ensures the drain slopes are correct from the start — establishing proper slope in drains added after slab pour is difficult without significant excavation.

## Backwater Valve Installation

If the existing weeping tile drains to the sanitary sewer (common in pre-1980 Toronto homes), disconnecting that drain connection and installing a backwater valve is most easily accomplished during the underpinning project when the main drain connection is already accessible.

The backwater valve prevents municipal sewage from backing up into the basement during peak flow events. Installation during underpinning is eligible for the City of Toronto Basement Flooding Protection Subsidy (up to $1,750 for the valve, and up to $2,800 for the drain disconnection).
